Sober Tuesdays (And Why They Never Stuck)

The on-again, off-again truth about trying to quit a version of yourself.

RB
Rama Ben Khalifa

I have quit partying more times than I can count, which is another way of saying I have never actually quit partying. Every 'this is it' lasted exactly as long as it took for the next invitation to feel more interesting than my own resolve.

The pattern always started the same way: a hard reset on a Monday, a spreadsheet-brained version of me who journaled and drank green tea and felt, briefly, like a completely different woman. By Thursday that woman was gone and I was back to being the one everyone actually expected to show up.

I didn't have an off-and-on relationship with partying. I had an off-and-on relationship with believing I deserved better than the chaos.

Type B on the surface, borderline underneath — I run hot and I run out. Discipline that isn't attached to something I actually feel doesn't survive contact with a Friday night. I know that about myself now, which is different from managing it, but it's not nothing.

This piece isn't a victory lap about finally sticking the landing. I'm publishing it because the honest, unglamorous middle — the part where you fail the same promise on a loop — is the part nobody puts in the caption, and I think it's the part that actually matters.
